Souvenirs on Kepler law....



O sweet memories! I remember very well the day, when I realised that origin is the focus of the ellipse which is formed by the section of the cone $x^2+y^2=z^2$ \footnote{$^{1)}$}{This day I knew the diagnosis on my eye.It was 30 March 2016...}

Here I will show it. More precisely we will show that equation $$ {x^2\over a^2} +{y^2\over b^2}=1\,. \tag{1} $$ describes an ellipse with foci at the points $F_i=\pm c$ where $c=\sqrt {a^2+b^2}$

Show first that statement (1) implies that for the ellpse $$\cases {x^2+y^2=z^2\cr z=kx+b\cr}\,. \tag{2} $$ the origin is one of its foci.

(We suppose that $|k|\leq 1$). Indeed one can see that the ellipse (2) can be rewritten in the following way $$ x^2+y^2=(kx+b)^2 \Leftrightarrow (1-k^2)x^2-2kxb=b^2\Leftrightarrow $$ $$ (1-k^2) \left(x^2-{2xkb\over 1-k^2}+{k^2b^2\over (1-k^2)^2}\right) =b^2+{k^2b^2\over 1-k^2}= {b^2\over 1-k^2}\Leftrightarrow $$ $$ (1-k^2)\left(x-{kb\over 1-k^2}\right)^2 +y^2={b^2\over 1-k^2}\,. \tag{3} $$ i.e. $$ {(1-k^2)^2\over b^2}\left(x-{kb\over 1-k^2}\right)^2 +{1-k^2\over b^2}y^2={b^2\over 1-k^2}\,. \tag{3} $$

Recall that the ellpse (1) $$ \left({x\over A}\right)^2 +\left({y\over B}\right)^2=1\,, \quad (A\leq B) \tag{1} $$ has foci at the points $(\pm c,0) $ with $C=\sqrt {A^2-B ^2}$

hence the ellipse (3) has foci at the points $$ F_{1,2}=-{kb\over 1-k^2}\pm \sqrt{A^2-B^2}= -{kb\over 1-k^2}\pm \sqrt{{b^2\over (1-k^2)^2}- {b^2\over (1-k^2)^2}}= $$ $$ -{kb\over 1-k^2}\pm {kb\over (1-k^2)}\,, $$ i.e. one focus is at the origin, and the second at the $x={2kb\over (1-k^2)}$.
