Let $\G\to M$ be groupoid, i.e. $\G$ is set of arrows, and $M$ set of unities; such that there are two maps $\bf s$ (sourse) and $\bf t$ (target maps from $\G$ to $M$, and there is a map $\bf u$ (unity map) from $M$ to $G$ and $$ {\bf s}\circ {\bf u}= {\bf t}\circ {\bf u}=\hbox {identity on $M$}\,. \tag{1a} $$ Denote by $\G^{(2)}$ subset of pairs of arrows in $\G\times\G$ such that the source of first arrow is the target of the second: $$ \G^{(2)}= \{(g,h)\colon\quad {\bf s}(g)= {\bf t}(h) \}\,. \tag{1b} $$ One can define on these pairs associative multiplication $\bf m$, $$gg {\bf m}\colon \G^{(2)}\to \G\,. \tag{1c} $$ such that:



1) for all $g,h\in \G^{(2)}$ $$ {\bf s}\circ {\bf m}(g,h)={\bf s}(h)\,,\quad {\bf t}\circ {\bf m}(g,h)={\bf t}(h) \tag{1d} $$ i.e. $$ {\bf s}\left(gh\right)={\bf s}(h)\,, {\bf t}\left(gh\right)={\bf t}(g)\,, \tag{1e} $$ for all $g,h\in \G^{(2)}$,

2) for all $g$ $$ g\circ \left({\bf u} {\bf s}\left(g\right)\right)=g \,, \left({\bf u} {\bf t}\left(g\right)\right)=g \,, \tag{1f} $$

3) if elements $g,h,f$ are companible, i.e. $t(f)=s(h)$ and $t(h)=s(g)$ then $$ g\circ (h\circ f)=(g\circ h)\circ f\,. $$

{\bf Remark} every thing is natural vice versa: sourse of arrow is the end of arrow, target of arrow is the beginning of arrow; one can say that we see $gh$ as composition of functions: $$ x{\buildrel h\over \rightarrow}y\,,\quad y{\buildrel g\over \rightarrow}z\,,{\rm then}\,\, {\bf t}(h)=y={\bf s}(g)\,,\quad {\rm and} \,\, x{\buildrel gh\over \rightarrow}z\,,\quad $$

{\bf Examples}

{\bf Example } Group $G$ is a groupoid

Let $G$ be a group. The groupoid $G\to \{*\}$ is just a group $G$.

Path groupoid Let $M$ be manifold. Consider groupoid ${\cal P}(M)M$ of all paths on $M$ with natural multiplication. If we identify paths by homotopy we will come to groupid $\Pi (M)$

{\bf Example } Action groupoid

Let group $G$ acts on the space $M$. Consider the action groupoid $\G\to M$ where set of arrows is $\G=G\times M$, and $$ {\bf s}(g,x)=x\,,\quad {\bf t}(g,x)=gx\,,\quad {\rm and} {\bf u}x=e $$





Groupoids $TG$ and $T^*G$

Action groupoids naturally produce groupoid structure on $TG$ and $T^*G$. To define groupoid structure on tangent bunlde $TG$, note that $TG$ can be identified with $G\times\GG$: $$ G\times\GG \ni (g,\xi)\mapsto \hbox {vector $\X\in T_{g}G$ corresponding to curve $\gamma_\X (t)=g(1+t\xi)$}\,. $$ Thus we come to groupoid $TG$ identifying it with action groupid $[G,\GG]$.

In the analogous way we define groupoid structure on $T^*G$

\bf Symplectic groupoid

Let $\G\to M$ be a groupoid. We say that a form $\sigma$ on $\G$ (of any rank) is multiplicative if $$ {\bf m}^*\sigma=\pi_s^*\sigma+\pi_t^*\sigma\,. $$ Let $\Omega$ be $2$-form on $\G$ (space of arrows) such that it defines symplectic structure on it (is closed and non-degenerrate).

We say that this groupoid is symplectic if the form $\Omega$ is multiplicative form.

{\bf Basic example}

Let $G$ be Lie group. Consider groupoid $T^*G\to \GG^*$ in {see Example above}.

Reall that we come to this groupoid identifying $T^*G$ with $G\times \GG^*$. Since The action of $G$ on $\GG^*$ defines action groupoid over $\GG^*$. Arrows of this groupod are pairs $(g,\theta)$ where $g\in G$ and $\theta\in\GG^*$, the source and target maps are $$ {\bf s}(g,\theta)=\theta\,,\quad {\bf t}(g,\theta)=Ad```^*_g \theta\,, $$ and unity map $\bf u$ such that ${\bf u}(\theta)=(e.\theta).$

One can see that tautological form (and hence its differential) becomes multiplictive form. Thus we define symplectic groupoid structure on $T^*G$. Recall what is it tautological $1$-form on $T^*M$.

Let $m\in M$ be an arbitrary point on $M$. and $\theta$ be linear function on vector space $T_mM$. Thus we come to an arbitrary point $\theta\in T^*M$ such that its projection $\pi T^*M\to M$ is equal to point $m$. Let $\X\in T_\theta \left(T^*M\right)$ be an arbitrary vector tangent to manifold $T^*M$ art the point $\theta$.

The point $\theta$ is projected on the point $m\in M$, thus the tangent vector $\X$ is projected on the vector $\pi_*\X$ which is attached at the point $m$ to the manifold $M$.

We are ready to define tautological $1$-form $\a$: $$ \hbox {the value of the form $\a$ on the vector $\X$ is equal to the value of the form $\theta$ on the vector $\pi_*\X$}\,. $$






{\bf Theorem} (Karasev, Weinstein,) Let $\G\to M$ be symplectic groupoid. Then maps $\pi_s$ and $\pi_t$ induce Poisson and anti-Poisson maps.

Why symplectic groupoid is so important?

Raison d'etre: Let $M$ be symplectic manifold. One can consider functional $$ \Psi_gamma(\X)=\int \L_\X \omega $$ One can see that $$ \Psi_{\gamma_1}(\X) =\Psi_{\gamma_2}(\X) $$ if paths $\gamma_1$ and $\gamma_2$ coincide at the starting and ending point. This means that functional $\Psi(X)$ can be well-defined on groupid $\Pi(M)$.
