On extended canonical transformaions



This is continuation of blog on 04 July.

Let $H=H(p,q,t)$ be Hamiltonian; a function $F=F(Q,q,t)$ a function (which obeys some conditions) generates extended canonical transformation $$ \pmatrix {p\cr q\cr}\mapsto \pmatrix {\rho\cr Q\cr} = \pmatrix {\rho(p,q,t)\cr Q(\rho,q,t)\cr}\,, \quad H(p,q,t)\mapsto h(\rho, Q,t) \tag{1a} $$ defined by condition $$ pdq-Hdt=\rho dQ-hdt+dF\,, \tag{1b} $$ i.e. $$ \cases { \rho_m=g_m(q,Q,t)= -{\p F(q,Q,t)\over \p Q^m}\cr p_i=f_i(q,Q,t)= {\p F(q,Q,t)\over \p q^i}\cr h=H+ {\p F(q,Q,t)\over \p t}\cr } \tag{1c} $$

Here we have to be (as usual) carefull with partial derivatives. E.g. first two equations in equation (1c) $$ \cases { \rho_m=g_m(q,Q,t)= -{\p F(q,Q,t)\over \p Q^m}\qquad (*)\cr p_i=f_i(q,Q,t)= {\p F(q,Q,t)\over \p q^i}\qquad (**)\cr } \tag{1d} $$ define extended canonical transformation $$ \pmatrix {p\cr q\cr}\mapsto \pmatrix {\rho\cr Q\cr} = \pmatrix {\rho(p,q,t)\cr Q(\rho,q,t)\cr}\,, \tag{2a} $$ and its inverse. Namely equation (**) in (1d) defines $Q^m=Q^m(p,q,t)$, then putting it in equation (*) of (1d) we define $\rho_m=\rho_m(p,q,t)=g_m(q,Q,t)\big\vert_{Q=Q(p,q,t)}$; thus we come to extended canonical transformation $$\cases{ \rho_m=\rho_m(p,q,t)\cr Q^m=Q^m(p,q,t ) }\,. \tag{2b} $$ Analogously equation (*) in (1d) defines $q^m=q^m(\rho,Q,t)$, then putting it in equation (*) of (1d) we define $p_i=p_i(p,q,t)= f_m(q,Q,t)\big\vert_{q=q(\rho,Q,t)}$; thus we come to inverse extended canonical transformation $$\cases{ p_m=p_m(\rho, Q,t)\cr q^m=q^m(\rho,Q,t) }\,. \tag{2c} $$ Our aim is to show straightforwardly that equation of motions remain the same if we perform canonical transformations, i.e. $$ \dot f = \{H,f\}_{I} = \{h,f\}_{II}\,, \tag{3a} $$ where $H=H(p,q,t)$ is initial Hamiltonian, $h$ is the new Hamiltonian (see 1(c)), and $\{f,g\}_{I}$ is canonical Poisson bracket with respect to initial coordinates $(p_i,q^j)$: $$ \{f,g\}_{I}= {\p f(p,q)\over \p p_i} {\p g(p,q)\over \p q^i} - {\p f(p,q)\over \p q^i} {\p g(p,q)\over \p p_i}\,, \tag{3b} $$ and $\{f,g\}_{II}$ is canonical Poisson bracket with respect to new coordinates $(\rho_m,Q^n)$: $$ \{f,g\}_{II}= {\p f(\rho,Q)\over \p \rho_i} {\p g(\rho,Q)\over \p Q^i} - {\p f(\rho,Q)\over \p Q^i} {\p g(\rho,Q)\over \p \rho_i}\,, \tag{3c} $$

Lemma Consider matrices of Jacobians for extended canonical transformation (2b) and and for inverse extended canonical transformation (2c) $$ A =\pmatrix { {\p \rho_m(p,q,t)\over \p p_i}\quad {\p \rho_m(p,q,t)\over \p q^j} \cr {\p Q^m(p,q,t)\over \p p_i} \quad {\p Q^m(p,q,t)\over \p q^j} \cr }\,,\quad B= \pmatrix { {\p p_i(\rho,Q,t)\over \p \rho_m} \quad { \p p_i(\rho,Q,t)\over \p Q^n} \cr {\p q^i(\rho,Q)\over \p \rho_m} \quad {\p q^j(\rho,Q)\over \p Q^n} \cr } \tag{4a} $$ then $$ {\p \rho_m\over \p p_i}= {\p q^i\over \p Q^m}\,,\quad {\p \rho_m\over \p q^i} =-{\p p_i\over \p Q^m}\,, \quad {\p Q^m\over \p p_j}= { \p q^j\over \p \rho_m}\,,\quad {\p Q^m\over \p q^i}= {\p p_i\over \p \rho_m}\,. \tag{4b} $$

Technical Remark I

To avoid confusions in calculation we have to choose rows and columns in matrices. One way to fix notations is the following: for every partial derivative ${\p \Pi_a\over \p S_b}$ we choose indices in a way such that indices in numerator are ROW indices, and index $b$ in denumerator are COLUMN indices. In this case equation (4b) reads $$ {\p \rho\over \p p}= \left(\p q\over \p Q\right)^T\,, {\p \rho\over \p q} =-\left({\p p\over \p Q}\right)^T\,, {\p Q\over \p p}= -\left({ \p q\over \p \rho}\right)^T\,, {\p Q\over \p q}= \left({\p p\over \p \rho}\right)\,. \tag{4c} $$ i.e. $$ A_{11}=B^T_{22}\,, A_{12}=-B^T_{12}\,, A_{21}=-B^T_{21}\,, A_{22}=B^T_{11}\,. \tag{4d} $$ Notice also that for $f_i={\p F(q,Q,t)\over \p q^i}$ and for $g_m=-{\p F(q,Q,t)\over \p Q^m}$

$$ \left(\p f\over \p Q\right)^T= -{\p g\over \p q} \tag{5} $$



Proof of lemma follows immediately from these relations.

E.g. $$ A_{11}={\p \rho\over \p p}={\p g(q,Q)\over \p Q}{\p Q\over \p p}\,, \, {\rm where}\, {\p f(q,Q)\over \p Q}{\p Q\over \p p}\, \tag{6a} $$ hence $$ A_{11}= {\p g(q,Q)\over \p Q}\left({\p f(q,Q)\over \p Q}\right)^{-1} \tag{6b} $$ and $$ B_{11}={\p q\over \p Q} \tag{6c} $$ with $$ 0={\p \rho\over \p Q}=0={\p g\over \p Q}+{\p g\over \p q} {\p q\over \p Q}\Rightarrow B_{11}={\p q\over \p Q}= -\left({\p g\over \p q}\right)^{-1} {\p g\over \p Q} \tag{6d} $$ Comparing $A_{11}$ with $B_{11}$ using (5) we see that $A_{11}=B_{11}^T$.

Analogously one can prove all relations in (4b).
