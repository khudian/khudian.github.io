Let $\AA$ be a space of formal functionals on $C^\infty(\R^N)$ which has the following expression $$ g\in C^\infty(\R^n)\,, \L(x,g)=L_0(x)+L_1(x,g)+L_2(x.g)+L_3(x,g)+\dots \tag{1a} $$ Here $\L(x,g)$ is a formal power series, a functional on $C^\infty(\R^M)$, $L_k(x,g)$ has an order $k$ over $g$: $$ L_k(x,g)=\int K(x,y_1,\dots,y_k) g(y_1)\dots g(y_k) dy_1\dots dy_k (1b) $$ and $\{K(x,y_1,\dots,y_n)\}$ are distributions.

We denote by $A_k$ the subspace of $A$ which possesses functionals of order $k$.

We denote by $\AA_k$ the subspace of functionals which do not possess terms of order $\leq k-1$: $$ \AA_k=\oplus_{i\geq k}A_i \tag{1c} $$

Definition Let $\L(g)$ be an arbitrary functional in $\AA$. A function $S_0(x)$ which is the value of this functional on the function $g=0$ will be called free function corresponding to the functional $\L(g)$: $$ \AA\ni\L(g) \hbox {corresponds a function}\,\, S_0(x)=\L(g)\big\vert_{g=0}\,. \tag{1d} $$





We say that the functional $\L$ has a local differential if there exist a map $y^a(x)=y^a(x,g)$ such that for an arbitrary function $h$ $$ \L(g+\vare h)-\L(g)= \vare h \left(y^a(x,g)\right)\,,\quad,(\vare^2=0)\,.) \tag{2} $$ Here a map $y^a(x,g)$ is a formal map: $$ y^a(x,g)=K^a_0(x)+ K^a_1(x,g)+ K^a_2(x,g)+\dots= $$ $$ K^a_0(x)+ \int K^a_1(x,y)g(y)dy+ \int K^a_1(x,y_1,y_2)g(y_1)G(y_2)dy_1dy_2+\dots (3) $$

Definition If $\L(g)$ is a functional with local differential then a map $K_0^a(x)$ corresponding to the functional $\L(g)$ in equation (3) will be called pillar map corresponding to functional $\L(g)$.









The following lemmas :

Lemma 1 Let $\L=\L(x,g)$ be an arbitrary functional in $\AA$ which has a local differential. Let $S_0(x)$ be a free function corresponding to this functional (see equation (1d)), and let a map $K^a(x)$ be a pillar map corresponding to this functional (see equation (3)). Then up to the second order $\L(g)$ is equаl to $S_0(x)+g(K^a(x))$, i.e. $$ \L(g)=S_0(x)+g(K^a_0(x)) ({\rm mod} \AA_2)\,,\quad {\rm i.e.}\,\, \L(g)-S_0(x)-g(K^a_0(x))\in \AA_2\,. $$
