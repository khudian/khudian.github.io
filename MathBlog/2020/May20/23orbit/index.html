here I represent the famous result about even dimensionality of the orbit of adjoint action on Lie coalgebra. However I will try to consider just its linear version



Let $V$ be $n$-dimensional vector space. For an arbitrary antisymmetric form $\Omega$ on this space denote by $H_\Omega$ the following subspace of $V$: $$ H_\Omega=\{V\ni\x\colon\,\,\Omega(\x.\y)=0\,, \hbox{for arbitrary $\y\in V$}\}\,. \tag{1} $$ One can see that dimension of $H_\Omega$ differs on dimension of $V$ on an even number. Indeed antisymmetric form $\Omega$ is non-degenerate on vector space $$ V'=V\backslash H_\Omega\,. \tag{2} $$ This implies that dimension of vector space $V'$ is even $$ {\rm dim}\, V'={\rm even}\,\,{\rm number} \tag{2a} $$ since determinant of anisymmetrical $n\times n$ matrix vanishes for odd $n$.

The statement that vector space $V'=V\backslash H_\Omega$ has even dimension is the source of the fact why orbits of coadjoint action of Lie group have even dimension (and they have natural symplectic structure.)

Indeed let $p\in \GG$ be linear for on Lie algebra. Consider a map $\sigma$ from Lie algebra $\GG$ to tangent space $T_p\GG^*$ which assigns to very vector $\xi\in \GG$ infinitesimal curve on $\GG^*$: $$ p(t)\colon \GG\ni \eta \langle\xi ,p(t)\rangle= \langle\ e^{t\xi}\eta e^{-t\xi} ,p\rangle \tag{3} $$ This curve begins at $p$, and the equivalence class of this curve defines tangent vector in $T_p\GG^*$. Thus we define the map $\sigma$ from Lie algebra $\GG$ to $T_p \GG^*$. $$ \sigma (\xi)=\left[p(t)\right]\,. \tag{4} $$

Now consider the antisymmetric bilinear form $$ \Omega=\Omega_p\colon\, \Omega_p\left(\X,\Y\right)= p\left(\left[\X,\Y\right]\right)\,. \tag{5} $$ One can see that vector space $H_\Omega$ defined by (1) coincides with kernel of the map $\sigma$ $$ \sigma (\xi =0)\Leftrightarrow p\left[\xi,\GG\right]=0\,. $$ Thus as it follows from (2a) $$ \hbox{dimension of orbit passing via the point $P$} = \dim T_p\GG^*= $$ $$ {\rm dim\,}\Im \sigma= {\rm dim\,} {\GG}- {\rm dim\,}\ker \sigma=\hbox {even number} $$
