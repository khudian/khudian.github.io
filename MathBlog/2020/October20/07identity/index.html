A larchik prosto otkryvalsia: on one unexpected identity



Today trying to calculate the action for `Kepler problem, I was trying to calculate $\int {dx\over (x+a)\sqrt {1-x^2}}$. For this purpose I was playing with the formula of taking derivstive of $\rm arcsin$ of fractional limear function. Thus I came to $$ \int {dx\over (x+a)\sqrt {1-x^2}}= {\rm arcsin }\, \left( {ax+1\over x+a} \right)\,, $$ and unexpectedly I came to the identity

$$ {\rm arcsin}{1\over x} ={\sqrt {-1}} \log \tan {\rm arcsin }\, \left( {x\over 2} \right)+{\pi\over 2}\,. $$ This formula lives in complex world. (It can be conisdered as analitical continuation for $\rm arcsin$. I never saw it, evidently I was excited, and.....

I wrote some manifestation of this formula in real world, and realised tht it is just a simple exercise in geometry.

Calculations are following: $$ {\rm arcsin}{1\over x} =\int \left( {\rm arcsin}{1\over x} \right)'+C=-\int {dx\over x\sqrt {x^2-1}}= \left(x={\rm ch}\theta\right)= -\int {{\rm sh\,}\theta d\theta\over {\rm ch}\theta {\rm sh}\theta }= $$ $$ -\int { d\theta\over {\rm ch\,}\theta }= -\int { d\theta\over {\rm ch\,}^2{\theta\over 2} + {\rm sh\,}^2{\theta\over 2} }= -\int { d\theta\over {\rm ch\,}^2{\theta\over 2} } { 1\over 1 + {\rm th\,}^2{\theta\over 2} }\,\, \left(u={\rm th}{\theta\over 2}\right) $$ $$ = - 2\int {du\over 1+u^2}= -2{\rm arctan\,}u+C'= - 2{\rm arctan\,} \left( {\rm th\,} \left({1\over 2} \left( {\rm arcch \,}x \right) \right) \right) +C'\,, $$ $$ {\rm th\,}{\theta\over 2}= {2{\rm sh\,}{\theta\over 2}{\rm ch\,}{\theta \over 2} \over 2{\rm ch}^2 {\theta\over 2} }= {{\rm sh\,}\theta\over {\rm ch\,}\theta+1}= {\sqrt {x^2-1}\over x+1}=\sqrt {x-1\over x+1} \, {\rm hence} {\rm arcsin}{1\over x}=-2{\rm arctg\,}\sqrt {x-1\over x+1}+C $$ Comparing LHS and RHS for $x=1$ we come to $C={\pi\over 2}$, i.e. $$ {\rm arcsin}{1\over x}= {\pi\over 2}- 2{\rm arctg\,} \sqrt{x-1\over x+1} $$ and this formula is stupiditly evident: consider triangle $\triangle ABC$ where $\angle C={\pi\over 2}$, $c=AB=x$ and $a=BC=1$. Let $BD$ be bissector of the angle $B$ where a point $D$ is on the side $AC$. Then $$ \angle A={\rm arcsin\,}{1\over x}= {\pi\over 2}-2\angle DBC\,, $$ and $\angle DBC={\rm arctg\,}{|DC|\over |BC|} ={\rm arctg\,}\sqrt{x-1\over x+1}$, since $|AD|:|DC|=x$. Thus the identity is proved.
