I heard all the life that Hamilton-Jacobi (HJ) method is the very powerful method, that using this method Jacobi aolved the particle on the ellipsoid. On the other hand I knew from student times that this method is intimately related with the function $S=S(q,t)$ which is a solution of equation $$ {\p S\over \p t}+ H\left(p_i= {\p S\over \p q^i},q^i,t\right)=0 \tag{0a} $$ it is this equation which is called Hamilton-Jacobi equation, and the function $S$ is nothing but action, but....

Stress the following.

i) yes, solution of equation (0) may be an action, or it may be one of its manifestations, or it may be.... E.g. consider $$ S={m(q^i-a^i)^2(2(t-\tau))}\,, \tag{0b} $$ and $$ \tilde S=a_i q^i-{ma_ia^i\over 2}t\,. \tag{0c} $$ these both functions, obey HJ equation for free particle, $H_{\rm free}={p^2\over 2m}$, the attentive student, will see that the action (0b) is the action of the free particle starting at the point $x^i=a^i$, at the moment $\tau$ and ending at the point $x^i=q^i$, at the moment $t$....

Very attentive srudent will see that second function is nevertheless is with Legendre trasnformation of the first one....

In HJ method you have to consider canonical transformation, which is generated by some (good function), this good function has to obey (0a)-like equation, and this is much more important that the fact that the function has resemblance with action, but does it needs to be an action?

The HJ method is the following:



Consider an arbitrary 'good' function which depends on time, and half of coordinates $p,q$ and the 'complimentary' half of coordinates $\rho, Q$, e.g. a 'good function' $F(q,Q,t)$, or a 'good' function $W(q,\rho,t)$) or a 'good' function $E(p,Q,t)$ or a 'good' function $G(p,\rho,t)$.

A function of this type defines an extended canonical transformation from canonical coordinates $(p,q)$ and Hamiltonian $H=H(p.q.t)$ to new canonical coordinates $(\rho, Q)$ and the new hamiltonian $h=h(\rho,Q,t)$.



i) for function $F(q,Q,t)$ we consider transformation such that $$ p_idq^i-H(p,q,t)dt=\rho_mdQ^m-h(\rho,Q,t)dt+dF\,, \tag{1.1a} $$ i.e. $$ p_i={\p F(q,Q,t)\over \p q^i}\,, \rho_m=-{\p F(q,Q,t)\over \p Q^m}\,, h(q,Q,t)=H(p,q,t)+F(q,Q,t)\,, \tag{1.1b} $$

ii) for function $W(q,\rho,t)$ we consider transformation such that $$ p_idq^i-H(p,q,t)dt=\rho_mdQ^m-h(\rho,Q,t)dt+dW-d(\rho_m Q^m) \tag{1.2a} $$ i.e.

$$ p_i={\p W(q,\rho,t)\over \p q^i}\,, Q^m={\p W(q,\rho,t)\over \p \rho_m}\,, h(q,Q,t)=H(p,q,t)+W(q,\rho,t)\,, \tag{1.2b} $$



iii) for function $E(p,Q,t)$ we consider transformation such that $$ p_idq^i-H(p,q,t)dt-d(p_iq^i)= \rho_mdQ^m-h(\rho,Q,t)dt+dE\,, \tag{1.3a} $$ i.e.

$$ q^i=-{\p E(p,Q,t)\over \p p_i}\,, \rho_m=-{\p W(q,\rho,t)\over \p \rho_m}\,, h(q,Q,t)=H(p,q,t)+E(p,Q,t)\,, \tag{1.3b} $$





iiii) for function $G(p,\rho,t)$ we consider transformation such that $$ p_idq^i-H(p,q,t)dt-d(p_iq^i)= \rho_mdQ^m-h(\rho,Q,t)dt+dG-d(\rho_m Q^m) \tag{1.4a} $$ i.e.

$$ q^i=-{\p G(p,\rho,t)\over \p p_i}\,, Q^m={\p G(q,\rho,t)\over \p \rho_m}\,, h(q,Q,t)=H(p,q,t)+W(q,\rho,t)\,, \tag{1.4b} $$

Notice now that the new Hamiltonian vanishes if one of the following Hamilton-Jacobi like equations is obeyed for generating function $F$ or $W$ or $E$ or $G$:





Remark The expression 'good' means that equations (*b) are not degenerate.

i) in the case (1.1), $$ {\p F(q,Q,t)\over \p t}+ H\left( p_i= {\p F(q,Q,t)\over \p q^i}, q,t\right)=0 \,,\quad (2.1) $$

ii) in the case (1.2), $$ {\p W(q,\rho,t)\over \p t}+ H\left( p_i= {\p W(q,\rho,t)\over \p q^i}, q,t\right)=0\,,\quad (2.2) $$



iii) in the case (1.3), $$ {\p E(p,Q,t)\over \p t}+ H\left(p_i=-{\p E(q,Q,t)\over \p q^i },q ,t\right)=0 \,,\quad (2.3) $$





i) in the case (1.4), $$ {\p G(p,\rho,t)\over \p t}+ H\left( p, q^m={\p G(p,\rho,t)\over \p \rho_m}, ,t\right)=0\,,\quad (2.4) $$

Thus we see that the problem is solved: vanishing of Hamiltonian means that new momentum and coordinates are constant: $$ h(\rho,Q,t)\equiv 0\Rightarrow {d\over dt}\rho_m=0\,, {d\over dt}Q^m=0\,, $$

This is important to emphasize again: an arbitrary solution of HJ equation serves as a generatig function: the most important is that hew hamiltonian vanishes!

Before considering few examples stress another very important feauter of HJ method, the feature which makes it very powerful:

Lemma Let $p_\mu=(p_0,p_i)$, $q^\mu=(q^0,q^i)$, ($i=1,\dots, n-1$) be $2n$ local coordinates in phase space, and $\varphi=\varphi(p_0,q^0)$ be a function of two variables, such that Hamiltonian $H=H(p_\mu.q^\nu)$ depends on coordinates $p_o,q^0$ through $\varphi$ $$ H\left(p_0,p_1,\dots,p_{n-1};q^0,q^1,\dots, q^{n-1}\right)= H\left(\varphi \left(p_0,q^0\right), p_1,\dots,p_{n-1};q^1,\dots, q^{n-1}\right)\,. $$ Suppose that there exists a function $A=A(q_0)$ such that $$ \varphi\left( p_0= {\p S\left( q_0\right)\over \p q_0 },q_0\right)=c\,, $$ where $c$ is a constant.

Then there exists a function $S(q^\mu,t)$ such that it is a solution of the HJ equation $$ {\p S\over \p t}+ H\left(p_i= {\p S\over \p q^i},q^i,t\right)=0 $$

such that $$ S(q^\mu,t)=A(q_0)+S(q^i,t). $$








Remark If a coordinate $q_0$ is cyclic coordinate i.e. Lagrangian does not depend explictly on $q_0$ $$ L=L\left(q_0,\dots q_{n-1},\dot q_0,\dots, \dot q_{n-1}\right) $$ then lemma may be obviously applied: for this Lagrangian $$ H_L=H\left(p_0,\dots,p_{n-1};q^1,\dots,p_n\right) $$ $\varphi=p_0$ and momentum $p_0$ is preserved. The lemma can be considered as generalisation of variables separation method.

Ted Voronov likes to quote the expression of Sergei Novikov who says that this lemma may be considered as the highest manifestation of separation of variables.






Example

Consider a particle in Coulomb field (Kepler problem) $$ L={m(\dot x^2+\dot y^2+\dot z^2)\over 2} -U\left(\sqrt {x^2+y^2+z^2}\right)\, \quad U=-{\a\over r}\,. $$ In spherical coordinates $$ H={p_r^2\over 2m}+ {p_\theta^2\over 2mr^2}+ {p_\varphi^2\over 2mr^2\sin^2\theta}+ {\a\over r} $$ Use lemma to solve HJ equation $$ S_t+{S_r^2\over 2m}+ {S_\theta^2\over 2mr^2}+ {S_\varphi^2\over 2mr^2\sin^2\theta}+ mgr\cos\theta+U(r)=0\,. $$ Notice that function $S=at$ is a solution of equation $S_t=a$, and a function $S=b\varphi$ is a solution of equation $S_\varphi=b$ (here $a$ ab=nd $b$ are constants), hence itfollows from lemma that we can look for the solution $$ S=at+b\varphi+A(r,\theta)\,. $$ Put this in equation we come to $$ a+{S_r^2\over 2m}+ {1\over 2mr^2} \left( S_\theta^2+ {S_\varphi^2\over \sin^2\theta} +mg \right) mgr\cos\theta+U(r)=0\,. $$
