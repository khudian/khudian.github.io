Groupoid of Batalin Vilkovisky: 2002--2017-2021



In 2002 with Ted we discovered BV-groupoid. It was the following:

Consider the solutions of equation $$ \Delta_\rho e^{\sigma\over 2}=0 \tag{1} $$

Denote $$ \rho'=e^\sigma \rho \tag{2} $$ Then $$ \cases { \Delta_\rho e^{\sigma\over 2}=0\cr \Delta_{\rho'} e^{\tau\over 2}=0\cr } \Rightarrow \Delta_\rho e^{\sigma+\tau\over 2}=0\,, \quad \hbox {(composition)} \tag{3a} $$ and $$ \Delta_\rho e^{\sigma\over 2}=0\Rightarrow \Delta_{\rho'} e^{-\sigma\over 2}=0 \quad \hbox {(inverse)} \tag{3b} $$ Show it based on my work with Peddie \footnote{${^1)}$} {this statement which was 'misteriously' formulated in LMP in 2002 follows from the Max Planck preprint in 2011 also, however the simplest proof follows from the work in 2011,}.

For an arbitrary volume form $\rho$ introduce operator on half densities $$ \Delta^{(\rho)}=\sqrt\rho\circ \Delta_\rho\circ{1\over \sqrt \rho}\,, {\rm and}\,\, \Delta_{(\rho)}={1\over \sqrt\rho}\circ \Delta^\rho\circ \sqrt \rho\,, \tag{4} $$ Operators $\Delta^{(\rho)}$ and $\Delta^{(\rho')}$ have the same symbol and they both are self-adjoint, hence they differ on scalar: $$ \Delta^{(\rho')}= \Delta^{(\rho)}+F(\rho',\rho)\,, \tag{5} $$ where $F$ is sero order operator. Apply operator (5) to semidensity $\sqrt {\rho'}$. We come to $$ \Delta^{(\rho')}\sqrt {\rho'}=0= \left[ \Delta^{(\rho)}+F(\rho',\rho)\,, \right]\sqrt{\rho'}= \left[ \Delta^{(\rho)}+ F(\rho',\rho)\,, \right] \sqrt{\rho}e^{\sigma\over 2}= $$ $$ \sqrt\rho\circ{1\over \sqrt \rho}\Delta^{(\rho)} \sqrt{\rho} e^{\sigma\over 2}+ F(\rho',\rho) \sqrt{\rho}e^{\sigma\over 2}\,. $$ This implies that $$ F(\rho',\rho)= e^{-\sigma\over 2} \Delta_\rho e^{\sigma\over 2} \tag{6} $$ Note that formula (6) coincides up the sign with cocycle $H(\rho',\rho)$ which was written in equation (2.3) in LMP. On this base prove (3a) We have from (3a) that $$ \Delta_{\rho'} e^{\tau\over 2}=0 \Rightarrow \Delta^{(\rho')}\sqrt {\rho'} e^{\tau\over 2}=0= \left[\Delta^{(\rho)}+F\right] \sqrt {\rho'} e^{\tau\over 2}= \Delta^{(\rho)}\sqrt {\rho'} e^{\tau\over 2}= $$ $$ \Delta^{(\rho)}\sqrt {\rho} e^{\tau+\sigma\over 2}= \sqrt\rho {1\over \sqrt\rho} \Delta^{(\rho)}\sqrt {\rho} e^{\tau+\sigma\over 2}=0\Rightarrow \Delta_\rho e^{\tau+\sigma\over 2}=0\,. $$ since $F(\rho',\rho)=e^{-\sigma\over 2}\Delta_\rho e^{\sigma\over 2}=0$ according to (3a). Now prove (3b).

We have to prove that condition $\Delta_\rho e^{\sigma\over 2}=0$ implies that $\Delta_{\rho'} e^{-\sigma\over 2}=0$. To prove this use (4) and (5). $$ \Delta_{\rho'}e^{-\sigma\over 2}= {1\over \sqrt {\rho'}}\Delta^{(\rho')} e^{-\sigma\over 2} {\sqrt \rho'}= {e^{-\sigma\over 2}\over \sqrt\rho} \left[\Delta^{(\rho)}+F(\rho',\rho)\right] \sqrt \rho=0\,, $$ since $F(\rho',\rho)= e^{-\sigma\over 2} \Delta_\rho e^{\sigma\over 2}=0$ due to the fact that $\Delta_\rho e^{\sigma\over 2}=0$ and $\Delta^{(\rho)}\sqrt\rho=0$: $$ \Delta^{(\rho)}\sqrt\rho=\sqrt\rho {1\over \sqrt\rho} \Delta^{(\rho)}\sqrt\rho= \sqrt\rho \Delta_\rho 1= \sqrt\rho {\rm div\,\,}_\rho{\bf D}_F\big\vert_{F=1}=0\,. $$
