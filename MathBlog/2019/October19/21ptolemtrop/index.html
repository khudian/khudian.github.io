Tropical analog of Ptolemy Theorem



If $a,b,c,d$ are lengths of the sides of the quadrangle which is inscribed in the circle, then $$ ac+bd=ef\,, $$ where $e,f$ are lengths of the diagonals of this triangle. THis is well-known Ptolemy's theorem \footnote{$^{*}$}{it becomes fashionable to quote this statemnet during talks in claster algebra}. Today Olga Kravhenko told me about the beautiful tropical analogue of this theorem[she learnt is in 1996 from Volodya Fock.



Let $ABCD$ be a quadrangle in the plane which is laminated by the system $\cal C$ of curves.

Denote by

$$ a-\hbox {the number of curves of the lamination $\cal C$ which intersect the side $AB$}\,, $$ $$ b-\hbox {the number of curves of the lamination $\cal C$ which intersect the side $BC$}\,, $$ $$ c-\hbox {the number of curves of the lamination $\cal C$ which intersect the side $CD$}\,, $$ $$ d-\hbox {the number of curves of the lamination $\cal C$ which intersect the side $DA$}\,, $$ $$ e-\hbox {the number of curves of the lamination $\cal C$ which intersect the digonal $AC$}\,, $$ $$ f-\hbox {the number of curves of the lamination $\cal C$ which intersect the diagonal $BD$}\,, $$ then in analogy with equation (1), there is the same tropical identity, ie. $$ \max \{a+c,b+d\}=e+f\,. $$
