For real case I know it from childhood, but for the complex case it is little bit fun: Today I did the proof which works for both cases.
CBH claims that $$ \langle f, f\rangle \langle g, g\rangle = ||f||^2 ||g||^2\geq |\langle f,g\rangle|^2 $$ Consider the following polynomial on $z=x+iy$: $$ P(z)=||f||^2||zf+g||^2=\langle f,f\rangle \langle zf+g,zf+g\rangle $$ It is not negative hence we have $$ 0\leq P(z)=||f||^2||fz+g||^2= ||f||^2 \left\langle fz+g\,, fz+g \right\rangle = $$ $$ =||f||^2 \left\langle \left\langle f,f \right\rangle z \bar z+ z\langle f,g\rangle+ \langle g,f\rangle \bar z+ \langle g,g\rangle \right\rangle = ||f||^2 \left( ||f||^2|z|^2 +z\langle f,g\rangle + \langle g,f\rangle \bar z+ ||g||^2\right)= $$ $$ \left\langle ||f||^2 z+\left\langle g,f\right\rangle\,, ||f||^2 z+\left\langle g,f\right\rangle \right\rangle +||f||^2||g||^2-\left|\langle f,g\rangle \right|^2\,. \eqno (*) $$ This implies that $$ ||f||^2||g||^2\geq \left|\langle f,g\rangle \right|^2\,, \eqno (**) $$ and $$ ||f||^2||g||^2=\left|\langle f,g\rangle \right|^2 \Leftrightarrow f || g\,. $$ Indeed if $f=0$, then this is obvious. Suppose that $f\not=0$. and choose $z=-{\langle f,g\rangle\over ||f||^2}$. Then the inequality (*) implies that $$ P(z)\big\vert_{z=-{\langle f,g\rangle\over ||f||^2}}= \left\langle ||f||^2 z+\left\langle g,f\right\rangle\,, ||f||^2 z+\left\langle g,f\right\rangle \right\rangle\big\vert_{z=-{\langle f,g\rangle\over ||f||^2}} +||f||^2||g||^2-\left|\langle f,g\rangle \right|^2= ||f||^2||g||^2-\left|\langle f,g\rangle \right|^2\geq 0\,. $$ We come to $(**)$.
