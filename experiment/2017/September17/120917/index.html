Orthogonal transfromations of $\E^2$ from the point of view of $\E^3$

Let $A$ be an orthogonal transformation of $\E^2$ changing its orientation, $\det A=-1$.
We know that $A$ has two eignevectors $\tilde\e,\tilde f$ with eignevalues $1,-1$. One can see it easily from three-dimensional point of view:
Consider natural embedding of $\E^2$ (with basis $\{\e,\f\}$) in $\E^3$, and consider the operator $\hat A$ such that $\hat A(\g)=-\g$.
Operator $\hat A$ is orthogonal operator and it preserves orientation. Hence it has axis $\N$: $A(\N)=\N$. It is easy to see that axis $\N$ is orthogonal to vector $\g$ $$ (\N,\g)=(\hat A(\N),-\hat A(\g))=-(\N,\g)\Leftarrow (\N.\g)=0\,. $$ i.e. $\N$ belongs to $\E^2$, thus we see that $A(\tilde\e)=\tilde\e$ for $\e={\N\over |\N|}$. If $\tilde \f$ is a vector orthogonal to $\N$ then $\hat A(\N)=A(\N)$ is also orthogonal to $\N=A(\N)$, but $A$ is not identical operator, hence hence $A(\tilde \f)=-\tilde \f$.
